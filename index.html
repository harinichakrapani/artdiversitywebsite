Why New York City's art houses aren't hiring diversity?

By Harini Chakrapani

New York City is perhaps the biggest melting pot of cultures and races, where over 4 million people of color are a majority. Yet, when it comes to arts and culture, the city shies from embracing diversity.

A recent survey of over thousand art institutions including museums, music and theater in the city, conducted by the Department of Cultural Affairs (DCLA) and a non-profit research organization Ithaka S+R revealed some shocking insights.
White non-Hispanics make up more than 60 percent of the cultural work force, while only 38 percent people of color are able to contribute to the growth and development of art in the city.
The inequity doesn't stop there. In the world of art, white (non-Hispanics) are leaders and decision makers while their colored counterparts represent a majority in the low paying, low skill security jobs.
According to the data, an overwhelming 78 percent of whites occupy positions of power in marketing, editorial, production, IT web development to serving as board members.

Voices from the city's art institutions representing minority cultures, believe the government's underfunding of their institutions, lack of paid art internships and expensive graduate arts program are the main reasons why minorities are discouraged and unable to advance to managerial roles and worse why culture specific institutions are dying a quick and silent death. Nicole Reiner, director of development at the Caribbean Cultural Center African Diaspora Institute in East Harlem, that celebrates creative voices of the underrepresented African diaspora said the DCLA had to extend its funding not just for arts programs offered by arts institutions but to operational costs that these institutions incur.
"Operational costs include everything from infrastructure, utilities to staff salaries,"she explained.

The data also revealed an interesting relationship between money and race. Bigger arts institutions- museums, galleries with bigger budgets amounting to millions of dollars, tended to hire more whites.



Like Nicole, Ali Rosa Salas also works for a culture specific institution, serving as the Exhibitions Coordinator at MoCADA, Museum of Contemporary African Diasporan Arts. While one of the primary reasons why she joined, was MoCADA's art diversity initiative, her background led to racial stereotyping by art employers outside MoCADA.

"People assume the kind of knowledge I have for contemporary art and dance based on my ethnicity and gender and how I choose to identify myself. Organizations try to pigeonhole [me] and assume that I know only black art, work by Puerto Rican artists and therefore assume that I cannot necessarily adapt,"she said.

New York City's mayor Bill de Blasio responded with a sense of urgency through a statement . "This survey gives us an opportunity to take a hard look at how we could do better when it comes to fostering a creative sector that opens doors for every New Yorker regardless of his or her background," he said.

Following the report, the DCLA announced a commitment of $1 million for cultural art institutions to support diversity efforts, additionally a donation of $2 million for the city's Theater Subdistrict Council for the training and development of theater professionals.
